2026 is arguably the darkest year for laptops<sup>[[citation]](https://www.bilibili.com/video/BV1Db5F6WE9u)</sup>. Unlike the 2022 crypto‑boom price hikes, this year the surge in AI computing demand has pushed hardware supplies entirely toward enterprises, causing consumer product price increases that already exceed the crypto‑boom. This year's college entrance exam takers face two choices: either pick an older model or go directly for the new ones. If a laptop is a real necessity and there are no price drops in sight, don't play the waiting game – otherwise, the rig you want will only get more expensive…

During this period of skyrocketing hardware prices, many manufacturers try to keep selling prices low to boost sales, but the cost is cutting storage configurations. For example, a gaming laptop with Ultra 9 275HX + RTX 5070 (default 16+1T) costs at least ¥12,000 RMB, while one with 16G RAM + 512G NVMe sells for around ¥8,000–9,000. Even so, AAA games occupy at least 90 GB of storage, so 512 GB can hardly "survive the future" :spoiler[~~but that's no reason to rush for 2TB; you can start with 1TB and add more later~~]. So choosing a laptop inevitably involves trade‑offs – summarised as "cut storage for performance, or cut performance for storage."

You ask me if "16G RAM" will be enough. Let me just say that in these times when RAM sticks are more expensive than gold, I personally wouldn't recommend jumping straight to 32 GB. You have to grit your teeth and live with 16 GB for the first couple of years, then add RAM at a lower price later. With [Mem Reduct](https://memreduct.app/) to optimise memory usage, it's plenty for gaming.

Now, as a soon‑to‑be freshman who already bought a gaming laptop, let me share my brief experience of selecting, purchasing, and using one.

## Why I chose the "Acer Predator Helios Neo 7"

One day after the college entrance exam, I had already watched tons of review videos and found none that satisfied me. Before that, I had also compiled my "avoid list". Let's see what remains in the final round:

1. **Lenovo Legion series**: Whether it's the 2025 R/Y 7000/9000 P or the 2026 model, the prices have skyrocketed (the nicknames "money grabber" and "premium grabber" are well‑deserved). Moreover, Legion has pitfalls like `aluminium heat pipes`, `black glue encapsulation`, `low‑temperature solder`, `low‑resolution low‑refresh‑rate screens`, etc. – overall, a complete disappointment.
2. **ASUS TUF series**: Last year ASUS's heavy promotion almost made me settle on the TUF Gaming A6 Pro, which was reasonably priced at that time (around ¥6,800 RMB). But this year my friend told me that online opinions have reversed – the TUF A6P has many quality control issues, and its price has also gone up. ASUS is a first‑tier brand, so I find this situation hard to accept, so I had to pass :spoiler[though the TUF Air 2025 might still be worth checking out; its online reviews are not bad, and the price with the national subsidy is acceptable, but the 2026 Air is really expensive.].
3. **Mechrevo**: As the joke goes, "once you buy a 'Geezer' (nickname for Mechrevo), you learn how to repair laptops." No matter which series you buy, the repair costs within a year or two can buy you a new one – too expensive, too expensive.)
4. **HP OMEN series**: The "black screen OMEN" (OMEN 9) incident is infamous. Although subsequent products fixed the issue, the after‑sales support still makes me stay far away.)
5. **MSI Stealth series**: Last year the MSI Stealth 16 "AI gaming laptop" was around ¥7,000. I was interested in AI training and waited for a while. At that price you could even get an RTX 5070. But now the price has more than doubled to over ¥10,000 – my family budget forces me to exclude it qwq.

With both first‑tier and second‑tier brands blowing up, I had to turn my attention to a brand rarely mentioned by well‑known reviewers: Acer (宏碁, pronounced "jī" in Chinese).

:spoiler[The character "碁" is an alternate form of "棋". In Taiwan it can be read as "qí" or "jī", but in mainland China only "qí" is standard. The official promotional video uses "jī", so I follow that pronunciation here.]

Acer may not be as mainstream, but its cost‑effective gaming laptop series, "Shadow Knight" (Predator Helios), is quite eye‑catching. Another series, "Predator" (掠夺者), targets high‑end e‑sports flagships – not necessary for most people.

Similar to how Lenovo Legion uses Y/R prefixes to distinguish Intel/AMD CPUs, Acer's Shadow Knight uses "Qing" (擎) and "Long" (龙) suffixes. A few days before the 618 sales, following a friend's recommendation, I decided to buy the Shadow Knight Long 7 :spoiler[with a Ryzen 7 260 CPU, ~~still the 7840 rebrand chasing me~~], which could be had for ¥6,800 with the national subsidy. But other group members and family advised against it, saying AMD laptops are poorly made – not only do they have heat accumulation issues, but also AMD's characteristic [PBO "turbo boost"](https://www.zhihu.com/question/556126847/answer/2711486575), fTPM affecting performance<sup>[[1]](https://sspai.com/post/72029)[[2]](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/566537204)</sup>, and the laptop often failing to wake from sleep, etc.

## Q&A

### 1. The CPU is a 14th‑gen Intel – is there a risk of degradation (shrinkage)?

Desktop 13th and 14th gen Intel CPUs introduced the hybrid architecture (P‑cores + E‑cores). However, at launch, Intel likely did not conduct thorough quality testing and parameter calibration, leading to a rapid decline in reliability and stability for end users. They reaped what they sowed.

According to "group statistics" and online discussions, while HX‑series mobile CPUs are ported from desktop, the i7-14650HX is a later Intel product with a lower risk of degradation. I've been using it for over a week with rare stuttering, and those instances are usually due to software optimisation rather than hardware issues.

### 2. Is the fan noise loud?

Online tests show about 50.2 dB during dual‑burn, which is quite loud. In my experience, switching to Silent mode via the control centre makes it very comfortable for office use – almost silent. In Entertainment mode, there's some fan noise. In Performance mode, the fans are clearly audible during gaming, but wearing a headset helps.

If you're very concerned about disturbing dorm mates with loud noise, I can only suggest not gaming at night or looking at thinner gaming laptops instead.)

### 3. Can the included SSD survive a disk scan?

What kind of abstract question is this?) In my opinion, most modern SSD controllers generally don't have major issues. Even if ACE scans constantly, it can probably last two or three years. If you're really worried, you can install Huorong and set custom rules to prevent ACE from scanning your drive to death.

### 4. I've heard the power never reaches its maximum – is that true?

During my dual‑burn test, it hit 145 W, while the theoretical maximum is 170 W – so yes, it doesn't reach the max. But dormitory power limits are usually strict, and the laptop has its own power limits anyway. Trying to push higher wouldn't achieve much. As long as you get smooth gameplay without frame drops, that's good enough.
